Share Market

The share market is a third option available to you for investing. Like property investments, the share market is considered risky - share prices regularly go up and down. The share market is typically even more risky than property.

The return from an investment in shares comes from the movements in the price of the shares, and dividends (which are a company's way of giving some of their cash profits back to shareholders).

As mentioned above, the share market is even riskier than property. Therefore, on average it returns more than a property investment. Otherwise no investor would be willing to take on the additional risk!

Things to be aware of in stockmarket investments include:

  • Some stocks pay large dividends. Other stocks pay no dividends at all. One isn't necessarily better than the other - if a stock doesn't pay dividends, it's stock price is higher by what it would have paid in dividends. Regardless, it is good to know whether a company likes to give its profits back to shareholders, or whether it likes to reinvest the profits back into itself.
  • The dividend policy of a company will affect it's share price chart. If a company pays out a $1 dividend, then their share price will usually instantly drop by $1. This makes sense - the company suddenly has less money in the bank, and so they are worth less. So just because one company's price line is a little under another company's line, it may not have performed any worse.
  • You will have to pay a transaction fee to both buy and sell your shares. This is typically around $10-$20 in Australia, though it may be more for big trades. This is why you should probably avoid buying share in very small increments. For example, if you buy $40 of ANZ shares with a transaction fee of $10, you have lost 20% of your money straight away! This could take a long time to make back. On the other hand, if you bought $500 of shares, the $10 is only 2% of your investment - you could make this back much quicker.
  • Big-name shares (e.g. Commonwealth Bank, ANZ, BHP) are traded a lot, by a lot of different people. This means it is usually easy to find someone to buy/sell shares with. Smaller companies are generally not traded as much, and therefore if you have to sell, you might have to take whatever price you can get.

Reasons you might consider investing in stocks include:

  • You want high returns, and are willing to bear more risk to get them.
  • If you want to invest in a higher risk investment (property or shares), but also want to be able to get your money out quickly in the future.

Reasons you might consider a different investment instead include:

  • You may need the money for other things in the near future. Even though you can buy and sell stocks quickly and cheaply, there is always the chance that a stock's price falls quickly. If you are forced to sell just after the stock's price falls, you may regret it.
  • You are investing for a short term only. Whilst on average stocks return more than savings accounts and property, they are also subject to much greater day-to-day fluctuations in value. A stock you buy could easily lose 5% or more in a week. If you are holding onto the stock for a long time, you minimise the effects of this up-and-down movement, but may not be able to in the short term.
