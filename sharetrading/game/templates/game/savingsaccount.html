Savings Account

One of the easiest things to invest in is a savings account with a bank. If you leave your money in a savings account for a fixed period of time, the bank will give you a little extra every so often. It gives you this extra because the bank uses your money for its own investing - the extra bit is a reward for you letting the bank use your money.

The return on a bank account is fixed - it doesn't depend on how the bank's investments perform. The bank will tell you upfront what return you will get (e.g. 2% a year). This would mean if you invested $100 at the start of the year, then at the end you would have $102 in your savings account.

The return is also usually quite small - only a couple of percent a year. This is because it is almost riskless - you will almost definitely get your money back at the end of the year.

The actual interest rate a bank will give you for a savings account or term deposit will depend on a lot of different factors.

Reasons you might consider investing in a savings account include:

  • It is very easy to do. Just head down to a local branch of a bank of your choice, and they will let you know what you need to do
  • It is very safe. Even if the your bank goes out of business, you will likely by covered by insurance provided by the Australian Government, meaning you will get your money back
  • It is usually easy to get you money out. If you ever need to spend your money, you can withdraw it very quickly. Note: some savings accounts (like term deposits) may have penalties for taking your money out early - these usually will give you a higher interest rate as a reward for keeping your money tied up for longer

Reasons you might consider a different investment instead include:

  • Small returns. Savings accounts usually offer much smaller average returns than other investments (such as property or shares). This is because they are so safe.
  • There may be some difficulty in getting your money out quickly. For example, if you need your money to pay for an unexpected car repair, you may have to pay a fee to get your money out of a term deposit
